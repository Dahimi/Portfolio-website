Quantum Computing

This is just a mockup article

Introduction

Computers have been around for decades, and over the past years new technologies have emerged, replacing their predecessors. Every year, we get a new CPU generation, smaller transistors, faster RAMs and so on and so forth. Most of those inventions are rather enhancement more than radical breakthroughs – Believe it or not, modern computers are still built upon the same layers as their precursors; But there is one technology that seems to be the next big thing, yeah you guessed it: Quantum Computers. Quantum Computing sounds cool and promising, since they rely on quantum mechanics principles that govern the world of atomic and sub-atomic particles.

Many people claim it is going to be the next market killing machine with its promising computing power.

Why do we need quantum computers in the first place?!

Computing power is growing at a very fast rate. Almost every 2years, our computers get twice faster and smaller. What used to be a room-sized computer is now equivalent to a laptop in terms of computational power. This is what call Moore’s Law.

That said, it might seem reasonable to think that a conventional computer could solve any computational task. It is just a matter of time – after all, we should just wait Moore’s law to do its magic. Well, it is not that easy!

Why? Because we presume that Moore’s Law will always be valid forever, whereas this is far from the truth. To ensure that Moore’s Law persists, transistors – which are the backbones of modern computers – have to get smaller and smaller. And in our essay to build thinner electronics we will inevitably hit an insurmountable problem; It is the hardware limits.

You see, classic computers rely under the hood on electronics, which in turn is based on classical physics law. The problem arises when our electronic components get to the size of an atom. In this case, quantum effects take over and our classical physic laws fall short.

If conventional computers could compute a variety of tasks, there is also a wide range of problems where they fall short. Those tasks take our best supercomputers long period of time to compute. And even if we suppose the validity of Moore’s Law, we cannot solve them in the foreseeable future.
